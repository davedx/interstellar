Interstellar Transport System

Author: Dave Clayton

Abstract

A system for interstellar transport to nearby stars is proposed utilizing current theoretically possible technology and engineering. It consists of two primary components: a fixed launch system used to accelerate spacecraft to relativistic speeds for interstellar transport, and an anti-matter beam core rocket capable of deccelerating to a stop at the destination.

Launch and Production Platform

The Launch and Production Platform (LPP) is a circular superstructure 8000km in diameter.

It is located at the Sun-Venus L4 Lagrange Point.

The LPP has two primary functions:

Launch

The launch system is an electromagnetically inducted slingshot device, with the following properties:

  • In acceleration mode, it is a closed loop. Objects are accelerated to relativistic speeds inside, completing many cycles before reaching their terminal velocity
  • In release mode, the launch hatch is opened, and the object inside is released on its outbound trajectory

Arbitrary launch velocities are supported by this design. This paper considers 0.5c as its base launch velocity.

Production

100km circumference proton particle accelerators are located along the interior rim of the superstructure at regular intervals. Anti-hydrogen is produced in the particle accelerators from positrons and anti-protons. There are 500 accelerators, each feeding into independent magnetic confinement storage systems.

Assuming an anti-matter conversion efficiency within the accelerators of 0.0001, the system can produce:

9 x 104g (90 kg) per year of anti-matter

Notes

The limiting factor on the LPP's minimum size is the acceptable radial acceleration imposed on the human passengers of a spacecraft accelerated to relativistic speeds. As the acceleration period is long, this is set to a maximum of 1.5g, implying a minimum radius at a maximum velocity of 0.95c equal to ~4000km.

The limiting factor on a spacecraft's mass is the amount of anti-matter that can usefully be produced in the pre-launch period.

Why not use an anti-matter rocket to accelerate too? The answer is because if we cannot escape the tyranny of the rocket equation, we can at least cut it in half. Future missions to construct a decceleration superstructure at other star systems could be interesting.

The LPP is powered from an array of solar panels tethered to the outer cirumference the superstructure. There are 5.03 x 1012m2 of solar panels. At a panel efficiency of 0.2, and a solar irradiance of 2622 W/m2, this provides a nameplate capacity not including system losses of 2.66 GW.

The LPP structure is not a continuous rigid body, but consists of many thousands of modules tethered to each other with cables. The LPP rotates, providing structural tension and stability, at 7 revolutions per day, imparting a centrifugal acceleration of 1m/s2 at the outer edge of the structure.

The LPP has no station keeping thrusters to maintain its orbit within the L4 Lagrange point. This results in a drift away from the sun of approximately 20km/year with its currently designed mass and structure.

Spacecraft Candidates

Interstellar probe

In order to facilitate a long-range interstellar probe, a spacecraft with a total mass including fuel of 100kg (50kg anti-matter propellent) is proposed. The probe would be accelerated to 0.5c by the slingshot. It would deccelerate at approximately 1.5 x 104m/s2 using its anti-matter rocket. This would result in a total mission duration to Alpha Centauri of around 10 years, with a pre-launch fuel production phase of 1 year.

Interstellar settlement fleet

It is more practicable for most kinds of settlement missions to be split into phases. For settlement of a planet in a different star system, a fleet of several spacecraft is proposed.

  1. Survey and power: the first spacecraft contains autonomous planetary probes (topographic, geological, biological, water and weather; communications), with seed manufacturing equipment for in-situ solar PV production. Cell and panel production begins as soon as a suitable site is located by the probes and the manufacturing equipment is landed.
    Mass: 5,000kg (2,500kg propellent).
  2. Manufacturing, power and habitat construction: the next three spacecraft (for redundancy and multiple landing sites) contain equipment for bootstrapping an industrial base, power generation and distribution, and habitat construction.
    Mass: 5,000kg each.
  3. Human transport: subsequent spacecraft transport humans alongside their consumable supplies such as food and other necessary materials, and higher technology components, that are not expected to be found on a distant planet or easily manufactured in-situ.
    Mass: 5,000kg each.

Anti-matter rockets with 2,500kg of propellent require 1,250kg of anti-matter, which can be produced in 14 years by the particle accelerator system. Travel time to a star 5 light years away at 0.5c is approximately 11 years (decceleration at 1g takes ~170 days).

Travelling mostly at 0.5c, the humans experience a subjective time of approximately 9.5 years due to time dilation (Lorentz factor of 1.15). It is possible to change the parameters of the human transport mission to allow a higher relativistic velocity, thereby increasing the Lorentz factor and subjective time. This would extend the total mission time in order to produce the extra necessary anti-matter.

Given an interstellar settlement mission with 5 human transports, the total mission time for the whole fleet to reach its destination is:

14 x 9 spacecraft = 126 years

Manufacturing and Construction

Key technologies

Manufacturing of the superstructure is a significant engineering and logistics undertaking. Most of the technology necessary is already at a high readiness level today, for example particle accelerators, solar PV, various electromagnetic induction and rail-based motive systems (for example maglev trains). However, some technologies are not commercialized, under-developed, or under-researched. For this project to be possible, the following technologies require more research and development:

  1. Anti-matter storage and distribution (magnetic confinement).
    While anti-matter has been successfully stored in confinement in laboratory conditions, it has only been extremely small amounts. Capture, long term storage and distribution from storage to spacecraft propellant tanks is one of the highest risk projects of the transport system.
  2. Superstructure module manufacturing and construction.
    This could be subdivided into constituent components, but it is sufficient to note that the ability of humanity to construct such a large structure is contingent on an extremely high degree of autonomous extraction, manufacturing, and construction. This means a diversified robotic fleet capable of operating in the extremely hostile environment of space with almost no human intervention over extremely long periods.
  3. Self-replicating robots.
    A dependency of the above is that the fleet of robots must be able to both repair itself and self-replicate. Without this key capability, the timelines for manufacturing the superstructure would be prohibitive given the limited launch capabilities of Earth to space, even with reusable rockets.
  4. Anti-matter (beamed core) rocketry.
    Critical to the entire concept of this transport system is that spacecraft launched at relativistic velocities must be able to deccelerate again. Very few propulsion methods known to be physically possible meet these requirements. Interstellar transport excludes almost all known propulsion except nuclear based systems: nuclear pulsed propulsion and anti-matter rocket variants. Anti-matter rockets have several challenges that are extremely difficult to solve (TRL 1), for example controlling the direction of thrust using some form of magnetic confinement, and ensuring annihilation is continuous and controlled the entire duration of the rocket's operation (almost half a year at 1g).

Many other new technologies and engineering methods will need to be researched, manifested and scaled up in order to build the superstructure, but the three listed are the most critical. Without significant progress, our current industrial and technological base will never let us reach the stars.

Timelines

Self-replicating robots are the key enabler of the construction of the superstructure. A robotic fleet is considered whereby each robot can replicate itself once per month. Each robot has a mass of 100kg, and can construct superstructure components at a base construction rate of 100kg/day.

Given the exponential growth of the robotic fleet, this results in a time to completion of the superstructure of:

16 months
